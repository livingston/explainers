Chapter I & II

Overview & Key Dates

The Income Tax Act, 2025 was enacted on 1st September 2025 as Act No. 30 of 2025. It consolidates and replaces the Income Tax Act, 1961 — a law that had been amended hundreds of times over 63 years and grown unwieldy.

ⓘ
Key Principle: The Act itself doesn't set tax rates. It provides the framework. Actual rates are set each year through the Finance Act (annual budget). The default rates are codified in Section 202 for individuals.

Section 6

Residential Status — Why It Matters

Your tax liability depends on whether you're a Resident, Not Ordinarily Resident (NOR), or Non-Resident (NR). Section 6 lays this out:

For Individuals

You are a Resident if EITHER condition is met:

  • You were in India for 182 days or more during the tax year, OR
  • You were in India for 60+ days during the tax year AND 365+ days in the preceding 4 years
⚠
Special cases (Sec 6(3)–(5)): Indian citizens leaving India for employment or as crew of Indian ships get the relaxed 182-day test only (not the 60-day test). Indian citizens/PIOs visiting India get 120 days instead of 60, if income exceeds ₹15 lakh.

Section 202 & Finance Act

Tax Slabs — New Regime vs Old Regime

ⓘ
The New Regime (Section 202) is now the DEFAULT. You don't need to do anything to be on the new regime. You must actively opt out to use the old regime.

Rebate under Section 156(2): If your total income does not exceed ₹12,00,000, you get a rebate of 100% of tax or ₹60,000, whichever is less. Effectively, income up to ₹12 lakh is tax-free under the new regime.

Standard Deduction: ₹75,000 for salaried individuals (Sec 19). So effectively, salary up to ₹12,75,000 is tax-free.

What You Give Up in the New Regime

Section 202(2) lists what you cannot claim:

  • HRA exemption
  • LTA (Leave Travel Allowance)
  • Most Chapter VIII deductions (like old 80C, 80D, etc.) — except NPS employer contribution (Sec 124) and Agniveer (Sec 125)
  • Interest on housing loan (for self-occupied property)
  • Set-off of house property loss against other income
  • Special depreciation / additional depreciation
  • Various industry-specific deductions (Sec 45, 46, 47, 48, 49)

The old regime rates (set by the Finance Act) for individuals below 60 years:

✨
Rebate under Section 156(1): Total income up to ₹5,00,000 gets a rebate of ₹12,500 — making it effectively tax-free.

Standard Deduction: ₹50,000 for salaried individuals.

What You Get to Claim

  • Section 123: Up to ₹1,50,000 (PPF, ELSS, LIC, etc.) — like old 80C
  • Section 124: NPS deduction up to ₹50,000 — like old 80CCD(1B)
  • Section 126: Medical insurance — like old 80D
  • Section 129: Education loan interest — like old 80E
  • Section 131: Donations — like old 80G
  • HRA exemption, LTA, home loan interest ₹2 lakh, and more

Sections 15–19

Salary Income

If you're a salaried employee, this is your primary head. Here's how it works:

What Counts as "Salary"? Sec 16

Section 16 defines salary broadly — it includes wages, pension, gratuity, commission, perquisites, profits in lieu of salary, leave encashment, and employer contributions to provident fund and NPS.

Perquisites Sec 17

Perquisites are non-cash benefits from your employer. Key ones that are taxable:

  • Rent-free accommodation (valued as prescribed)
  • Concessional-rate accommodation
  • Company car, stock options (ESOPs), club memberships
  • Employer contributions to PF/NPS/superannuation exceeding ₹7,50,000/year combined (Sec 17(1)(h))
Sections 20–22

Income from House Property

Self-Occupied Property Sec 21(6)–(7)

Up to 2 self-occupied properties can have nil annual value. This means if you own two homes and live in them (or can't occupy due to employment etc.), no tax on their "deemed rent".

Let-Out Property

For rented-out properties:

Under the New Regime: Loss from house property cannot be set off against other heads (Sec 202(2)(b)(ii)). Under the old regime, up to ₹2 lakh of housing loss can be set off.

Profits & Gains of Business or Profession

This is the largest section of the Act — covering everything from depreciation to presumptive taxation.

Key Deductions for Business

Depreciation Sec 33

Depreciation on tangible and intangible assets is allowed on the Written Down Value (WDV) of "block of assets". Rates are prescribed by rules. Additional depreciation and accelerated depreciation provisions exist for manufacturing and power generation.

Scientific Research Expenditure Sec 45

Both capital and revenue expenditure on scientific research related to the business are deductible. For biotech/manufacturing: 100% of R&D expenditure. Donations to approved research bodies get weighted deductions.

Preliminary Expenses Sec 44

Expenditure on project reports, feasibility studies, company incorporation — amortized equally over 5 years.

Sections 67–91

Capital Gains

Any profit from selling a "capital asset" is a capital gain. The Act distinguishes between short-term and long-term based on holding period.

Holding Period Sec 2(101)

All other assets (property, gold, unlisted shares, debt MFs, etc.)24 months24 months

Capital Gains Tax Rates

TypeApplicable ToRateSection
STCG (Special)Listed equity shares, equity MFs (STT paid)20%Sec 196
STCG (General)All other short-term assetsAs per slab rate
LTCG (General)Listed equity, equity MFs (above ₹1.25 lakh)12.5%Sec 198

✨
LTCG Exemption: Long-term capital gains on listed equity/equity MFs up to ₹1,25,000 per year are exempt (Sec 198).

Indexation: The indexation benefit (adjusting purchase price for inflation) has been removed for assets acquired after 23 July 2024 in exchange for the lower 12.5% rate.

Exemptions on Capital Gains

Reinvestment in Residential Property Sec 78

LTCG from selling a residential house can be exempt if you buy/construct another residential house within the prescribed period. The new house must be held for at least 3 years. Exemption is capped at ₹10 crore.

Investment in Capital Gains Bonds Sec 79

LTCG from any asset can be exempt if invested in specified bonds (like NHAI, REC) within 6 months. Maximum ₹50 lakh in a financial year. Lock-in: 5 years.

Compulsory Acquisition of Land Sec 82

If agricultural land (in certain areas) is compulsorily acquired by the government and you invest the compensation in specified assets within 2 years, the capital gain is exempt.

Sections 92–95

Income from Other Sources

This is the residual head — any income not covered under the other four heads falls here.

Common Examples

  • Interest income (savings bank, FDs, bonds)
  • Dividend income
  • Gifts received (above ₹50,000 from non-relatives)
  • Rental income from sub-letting
  • Winning from lotteries, games, betting Sec 194
  • Family pension
  • Agricultural income from outside India
⚠
Winnings (Sec 194): Income from lotteries, crossword puzzles, races, card games, gambling, online gaming, and similar is taxed at a flat 30% — no deductions, no exemptions.

Schedule XV lists the investments/expenses that qualify (aggregate limit ₹1,50,000):

Rebates & Reliefs

ⓘ
Marginal Relief (Sec 156(2)(b)): If your income slightly exceeds ₹12 lakh, you won't pay more total tax than the amount your income exceeds ₹12 lakh. Example: If income is ₹12,10,000 — your tax won't exceed ₹10,000.

Relief for Arrear Salary Sec 157

If you received salary arrears or advance salary pushing you into a higher slab, you can apply for relief. The tax department recalculates as if the income was spread over the relevant years.

Double Taxation Relief Sec 159–160

If you've paid tax on income in another country that also taxes it in India, you get relief through DTAA agreements (Section 159) or unilateral relief (Section 160).

Chapter XV — Sections 263–268

Filing Your Income Tax Return

Who Must File? Sec 263

Every person whose total income exceeds the basic exemption limit must file a return. Even if your income is below the limit, you must file if you want to:

  • Claim a refund
  • Carry forward losses
  • Meet specific criteria (e.g., foreign assets, expenditure thresholds)

Filing Deadlines

CategoryDue Date
Individuals / HUFs (no audit)31st July of the tax year following
Businesses requiring audit31st October of the following year
Transfer pricing cases30th November of the following year
Belated return (late filing)31st December of the following year
Updated returnWithin 24 months from end of relevant tax year (with additional tax)

Updated Return Sec 263(3)

You can file an "updated return" within 24 months of the end of the tax year — even after the belated deadline — if you have additional income to declare. This comes with additional tax of 25% (if filed within 12 months) or 50% (12–24 months) of the aggregate tax and interest.

Prosecution — Criminal Offences

⚠
Chapter XXII deals with criminal prosecution for tax evasion. Key offences include:
  • Wilful attempt to evade tax — imprisonment up to 7 years
  • Failure to file return — imprisonment up to 7 years (if tax evaded > ₹25 lakh)
  • False statements / withholding information — imprisonment up to 2 years
  • Undisclosed foreign income — imprisonment up to 10 years
Chapter XI — Sections 178–186

General Anti-Avoidance Rule (GAAR)

GAAR gives the tax department power to declare any "arrangement" as an impermissible avoidance arrangement if its main purpose is to obtain a tax benefit.

When Can GAAR Apply? Sec 179

An arrangement is impermissible if its main purpose is tax benefit AND it:

  • Creates abnormal rights/obligations not seen in arm's length dealing
  • Results in misuse or abuse of the Act's provisions
  • Lacks commercial substance (Sec 180)
  • Is carried out in a manner not ordinarily employed for genuine purposes
⚠
Practical Impact: GAAR is a powerful weapon for the tax department. If invoked, the tax consequences of the entire arrangement can be recharacterized. However, it requires approval from a Principal Commissioner-level panel (Sec 184) and cannot be applied lightly.

What You Need To Do

Action Items Checklist

Based on everything above, here's what you should do:

For Salaried Individuals

  • Decide: New or Old Regime? — Use the calculator above. If your deductions (80C, 80D, HRA, home loan) exceed ~₹3.75 lakh, old regime might save more. Otherwise, new regime wins.
  • Inform your employer about your regime choice at the start of the financial year so TDS is deducted correctly.
  • Ensure PAN-Aadhaar linkage — your PAN will become inoperative without it.
  • Review your Form 16 — it will use new section numbers. Know the mapping (80C → Sec 123, etc.).
  • File by 31st July of the following year.
  • Claim the ₹75,000 standard deduction (new regime) or ₹50,000 (old regime).
  • Verify TDS in Form 26AS / AIS — ensure all TDS matches your records.

For Freelancers & Professionals

  • Check if presumptive taxation (Sec 58) applies — gross receipts up to ₹50 lakh (₹75 lakh if cash < 5%) for professionals.
  • Maintain books of account if income > ₹2,50,000 or turnover > ₹25 lakh (Sec 62).
  • Get tax audit done if turnover exceeds ₹1 crore (₹10 crore with low cash transactions) — Sec 63.
  • Pay advance tax quarterly if expected tax liability exceeds ₹10,000 for the year.
  • Choose your regime — once you opt out (old regime) with business income, you can switch back only once.

For Investors

  • Track holding periods carefully — 12 months for listed equity, 24 months for everything else.
  • Utilize the ₹1.25 lakh LTCG exemption on listed equity/equity MFs.
  • No indexation for new acquisitions — factor the flat 12.5% rate into investment decisions.
  • Harvest losses before year-end to set off against gains (STCL against STCG/LTCG, LTCL against LTCG only).
  • STCG on equity is now 20% — consider holding for 12+ months when possible.
  • Crypto investors — 30% flat tax, no deductions, 1% TDS on transfers.

For Business Owners

  • Review company tax rate options — Sec 200 (22%), Sec 201 (15% for new manufacturing), or standard rates.
  • Cash transaction limit — no payment above ₹10,000/day in cash to a single person (Sec 188).
  • Transfer pricing compliance — Sec 161–172 for international/specified domestic transactions.
  • Be GAAR-aware — any arrangement primarily aimed at tax benefit may be challenged.

For NRIs

  • Track your days in India — 182-day and 60/120-day rules (Sec 6).
  • Income earned/received in India is always taxable regardless of status.
  • Claim DTAA benefits — Sec 159 allows relief for double-taxed income.
  • TDS on Indian income — ensure proper TDS certificates and claim refunds if over-deducted.
